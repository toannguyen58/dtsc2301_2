The Siren — US Recession Early Warning System

May 2026

Full project code, pipeline, and output charts are available on GitHub: https://github.com/ericnguyen58/chess-end-to-end

1. Project Overview

The Siren is an early-warning system for US recessions. It watches 9 economic indicators every month and outputs a single number — the probability of a recession beginning within the next 6 months — color-coded so anyone can read it at a glance.

ColorProbabilityWhat it means
Green< 25%Economy looks healthy
Yellow25–50%Some warning signs, worth watching
Orange50–75%Significant recession risk
Red> 75%Recession likely imminent

Why 6 months? That's the practical window for business decisions — adjusting hiring plans, cutting capital expenditure, building cash reserves. Shorter lead times leave no room to act. Longer lead times lose accuracy.

As of August 2024, The Siren is at 9.7%. [Green]

2. The Data

Nine economic series covering 60+ years of US history (1960–2024), drawn from three sources: the Federal Reserve (FRED), the IMF, and the OECD.

Ground truth labels come from the National Bureau of Economic Research (NBER) — the official US arbiter of recession timing — pulled from FRED as a binary 0/1 series.

3. Feature Engineering

Making the Data Stationary

Raw economic levels aren't comparable across time. A CPI reading of 300 in 2020 means something entirely different from 30 in 1970. Five level series were transformed to month-over-month percentage change (industrial production, CPI, real income, prime rate, business inventories). This removes long-run trends and makes each series stationary — a statistical requirement for reliable time-series modeling. All five passed ADF (Augmented Dickey-Fuller) tests after the transformation, having failed them in levels.

The remaining four series (consumer confidence, unemployment, yield spread, building permits) were kept as-is — they are already stationary or near-stationary in levels.

Yield Spread Lags

The inverted yield curve's predictive power peaks with a delay — the economy doesn't react instantly to a rate inversion. Lags at 3, 6, and 12 months were added as explicit features, giving the model three separate windows to learn this relationship without manual feature selection. 

Final feature set — 17 columns:

  consumer_conf       unemployment        yield_spread        building_permits
  indpro_level        cpi_inflation_level real_dpi_level      prime_rate_level
  biz_inventories_level  indpro          cpi_inflation        real_dpi
  prime_rate          biz_inventories     T10Y3M_lag3         T10Y3M_lag6
  T10Y3M_lag12

The Prediction Target

The model predicts recession_6m: a binary label that is 1 if any of the next 6 months falls inside an NBER recession. This means the model predicts the probability of a recession starting or continuing within the next half-year — not a point-in-time call. Roughly 20% of historical months carry a positive label, reflecting that recessions are relatively rare.

4. Validation Strategy

A naive train/test split on time-series data leaks the future into training. The Siren uses walk-forward (expanding window) validation — the model is trained only on past data, then tested on future data it has never seen: 

[Train: 1960–1994] → 6-month gap → [Test: 1994–2004]   Fold 1
[Train: 1960–2004] → 6-month gap → [Test: 2004–2014]   Fold 2
[Train: 1960–2014] → 6-month gap → [Test: 2014–2024]   Fold 3

The 6-month gap between training and testing is critical: because recession_6m[t] encodes information from recession[t+1] through recession[t+6], removing the gap would allow the last training month's label to overlap with the first test prediction — a form of data leakage that would inflate performance.

The expanding window (rather than a rolling window) retains all historical data. The 1960s recessions are rare but valuable signal, and there's no reason to discard them when predicting the 2010s.

5. The Model — XGBoost with F-Beta Threshold Optimization

Why XGBoost?

The Siren's primary model is XGBoost, a gradient-boosted decision tree algorithm. It builds hundreds of shallow trees in sequence, each correcting the errors of the last. This handles non-linear relationships and feature interactions well — important because economic dynamics are rarely simple. 

XGB config:
  n_estimators: 300  |  max_depth: 4  |  learning_rate: 0.05
  subsample: 0.8     |  colsample_bytree: 0.8
  scale_pos_weight = neg_count / pos_count  (computed per fold)

Threshold Optimization

The default 0.5 threshold isn't appropriate here. A missed recession is far more costly than a false alarm. The threshold was optimized per fold using F-beta with β=2, which weights recall twice as heavily as precision: 

F_β = (1 + β²) × (precision × recall) / (β² × precision + recall)
     with β=2: recall contributes 4× more weight than precision

A grid search across 199 thresholds (0.01–0.99) finds the cutoff that catches the most recessions even at the cost of more false alarms.

6. Model Comparison

Three models were evaluated on the same walk-forward splits with identical F-beta threshold optimization to test whether XGBoost's complexity was justified.

ModelAUC-ROCRecallPrecisionF1
XGBoost0.8660.9160.3690.508
Random Forest0.8400.9420.3360.489
Logistic Regression0.8910.9860.3580.509

The most important finding: Logistic Regression outperforms XGBoost on AUC, Recall, and F1. This isn't a failure — it's a finding. The inverted yield curve has a fundamentally linear relationship with recession risk. When a simpler model captures the signal just as well, that simpler model is often the better choice.

XGBoost was kept as the primary model for two reasons: best precision (fewest false alarms) and best calibrated ECE after Platt scaling — its per-bin probabilities are the most trustworthy. For a warning system where false alarms erode trust, this distinction matters.

7. Calibration — Are the Probabilities Trustworthy?

A model that says "70% recession risk" should be right about 70% of the time in similar situations. If it's only right 30% of the time, the probability isn't calibrated and shouldn't be used as a literal forecast. This is measured with Expected Calibration Error (ECE) — lower is better.

Each model received its own independent Platt scaler — a logistic regression fitted on the model's raw probability outputs using folds 1+2, then evaluated on fold 3. Applying one model's calibrator to another's output would be invalid because each model's probability distribution is fundamentally different.

All three models improved after Platt scaling. Logistic Regression had the worst raw calibration (it produces very extreme probabilities near 0 and 1) but after calibration it achieves the best Brier score (0.060). XGBoost achieves the best calibrated ECE (0.118) — its probability buckets are the most reliable. A naive "always predict base rate" model scores ~0.146 on the Brier metric; all three models beat that comfortably.

8. Did It Catch Every Recession?

Out-of-Sample Results (the only fair evaluation)

RecessionFirst alarmLead timePeak probability
Dot-com / 9-11 (2001)Oct 20006 months30.1%
Great Recession (2008)Jul 20076 months99.8%
COVID-19 (2020)Sep 20196 months76.8%

3 out of 3. Every out-of-sample recession caught with a full 6 months of lead time.

The Two Interesting Cases

2001 — peak 30.1%: The recession formed gradually as the dot-com bubble deflated through 2000. Then 9/11 hit as an external shock — impossible to read from macro indicators. The model correctly detected the pre-existing macro weakness and fired at 30%. It cannot predict terrorist attacks, nor should it be expected to.

2020 — peak 76.8%: The alarm fired in September 2019, six months before COVID. The signal came from the inverted yield curve, which had been flashing red since mid-2019 due to Fed policy and trade war uncertainty. COVID exploited the economic vulnerability the model was already reading.

The Siren reads economic vulnerability, not the unpredictable events that exploit it. That is exactly what an early-warning system should do.

False Alarm Rate

63 false alarms out of 317 non-recession test months — a 19.9% false alarm rate. Expect roughly one spurious signal every 5 calm months. This is the deliberate cost of catching 91.6% of pre-recession months. The threshold was tuned to accept this tradeoff explicitly via F-beta (β=2).

9. Explainability — Why Is the Siren at X%?

A probability is only useful if you can explain it. The Siren uses SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) to decompose every prediction into the contribution of each feature. SHAP goes beyond feature importance rankings — it shows the directional, per-prediction contribution of each feature in the units of the model output.

10. Limitations

  • Exogenous shocks are invisible. Pandemics, wars, and financial fraud don't appear in macro data before they strike. The model reads economic vulnerability, not the events that exploit it.
  • Small out-of-sample test set. Three recessions in 30 years is thin. With n=3, results could partly reflect luck — error bars are wide.
  • No hyperparameter tuning. XGBoost parameters were set by hand. A grid search within each fold could improve performance further.
  • Calibration is imperfect. ECE of 0.118 after Platt scaling is better than raw but not perfect. Treat probabilities as ranking signals rather than literal forecasts.
  • Data vintage. NBER recession labels are announced months after the fact. In live deployment, the most recent months would carry uncertain labels.

  • The output is a signal, not a verdict — and wide distribution creates a causation risk. If The Siren were broadly published and acted upon at scale, it could become part of the very mechanism it is trying to detect. A widely circulated "75% recession probability" triggers precautionary spending cuts, hiring freezes, and capital flight — each of which depresses the economy independently of whatever the underlying indicators were actually saying. The model would stop being a passive observer and start being a cause. This is the economic equivalent of a fire alarm that starts fires: the prediction accelerates the outcome it predicts. The Siren should be used as one private input into a decision, not broadcast as a public forecast.

The most important finding isn't the AUC score. It's that a simple linear model matches a complex tree model on this problem — because the yield curve's relationship to recessions is fundamentally linear. A rigorous evaluation surfaces that insight rather than hiding it behind complexity.
